Every stock on MomentumRadar shows five performance figures: 1D, 1W, 1M, 3M and 1Y. Each is simply the percentage change in the share price over that period, using daily closing prices adjusted for dividends and splits. Individually they're trivia; together they form a picture of how a trend is developing.

What each timeframe tells you

1D (one day) is mostly noise — news headlines, index rebalancing, a big fund adjusting a position. Never judge a stock by one day. We give it only 10% weight in the Momentum Score for exactly this reason.

1W (one week ≈ 5 trading days) starts to show short-term direction, but is still heavily influenced by single events like an earnings report.

1M (one month ≈ 21 trading days) is the sweet spot for spotting a developing trend early. It's long enough to filter daily noise, short enough to catch changes before everyone else's quarterly review does. It carries our highest weight: 30%.

3M (one quarter ≈ 63 trading days) confirms whether the monthly move is a real trend or a blip. A stock up over both 1M and 3M is being consistently bought.

1Y (one year ≈ 252 trading days) is the big picture: is this a long-term winner or a beaten-down stock having a bounce? Academic momentum research is largely built on 12-month lookbacks.

Patterns worth noticing

All five green — consistent accumulation across every horizon. This is the classic strong-momentum profile, and it's what pushes a Momentum Score above 80. The caution: the longer and steeper the run, the more the stock depends on continued good news.

Long green, short red (1Y strongly positive, 1W/1M negative) — an uptrend taking a breather, or the start of a reversal. Watch the 90-day Momentum Score chart: a score drifting down for weeks while price plateaus is often the earliest visible sign that a trend is tiring.

Short green, long red (1M positive, 1Y negative) — a possible turnaround attempt. These are tempting and dangerous: some become great recoveries, many are "dead-cat bounces". This profile scores mid-range on purpose.

The acceleration check — compare the 1M figure (roughly ×12 to annualise) against the 1Y figure. If the recent pace is far above the yearly pace, momentum is accelerating; if far below, it's fading even while all numbers are green. Three mistakes beginners make with these numbers

First, chasing the 1D column — buying whatever jumped the most today, which is closer to gambling than investing. Second, ignoring currency — a Japanese stock up 20% in yen may be up much less in your home currency. Third, treating past returns as forecasts — every figure on this site describes what already happened. The momentum effect says trends persist on average; it says nothing about any single stock this week. That's why every page here links to the company's financial statements: the numbers behind the price are the ones that decide who's still standing after the trend ends.
