MomentumRadar tracks 13 markets across four regions. Each has its own personality — knowing it helps you interpret why momentum shows up where it does.

United States (NYSE & NASDAQ)

The world's largest market and the pace-setter for everyone else. Deep liquidity, the biggest technology companies on earth, and the home of most global momentum leadership. US trading hours (09:30–16:00 New York time) mean American moves are reacted to by Asia the following morning.

United Kingdom (FTSE 100)

London's blue-chip index is heavy in energy, mining, banks and consumer staples — businesses that earn mostly outside the UK. It often moves with commodity prices, and its dividend yields are among the highest of any developed market.

Germany (DAX) and France (CAC 40)

Europe's two industrial giants. The DAX leans engineering, autos, chemicals and increasingly defence; the CAC is powered by global luxury (LVMH, Hermès), aerospace and energy. Both trade 09:00–17:30 Central European Time and respond strongly to global trade conditions.

Australia (ASX 200)

A market of miners and banks, with world-class healthcare and software names alongside. Because its giant resource companies sell iron ore and gas to Asia, the ASX is often a barometer of Chinese demand.

Japan (Nikkei 225)

Asia's largest developed market. Corporate-governance reform, a historically weak yen and world-leading factory-automation and semiconductor-equipment firms have made Tokyo a global momentum story in recent years. Yen moves matter: a falling yen typically boosts exporter shares.

Hong Kong (Hang Seng)

The gateway between global capital and Chinese companies — Tencent, Alibaba, Meituan, BYD. It is among the most policy-sensitive major markets: announcements from Beijing can move the index several percent in a session, which makes momentum here powerful but volatile.

Singapore (STI)

A stable, bank-dominated market (DBS, OCBC and UOB are roughly half the index) and Asia's REIT capital. Less explosive than its neighbours, favoured by income investors.

The ASEAN five: Malaysia, Indonesia, Thailand, Philippines, Vietnam

Bursa Malaysia mixes banks, plantations and energy with a recent data-centre construction boom. Indonesia (IDX) is Southeast Asia's largest economy: big banks plus world-scale nickel and coal producers make it consumer growth and commodity cycle in one index. Thailand (SET) revolves around energy, tourism recovery and medical tourism. The Philippines (PSEi) is dominated by family conglomerates and a young, consuming population. Vietnam (HOSE) is the frontier-market star: manufacturing relocation, rapid urbanisation and a possible upgrade to emerging-market status — with the region's biggest swings in both directions.

Why time zones matter to momentum watchers

Markets open in a relay: Asia first, then Europe, then the US. Strong momentum in US technology on Monday often echoes through Taiwanese, Japanese and ASEAN suppliers on Tuesday. When you see a theme leading in one region on MomentumRadar, checking the related markets is often where the insight is.
