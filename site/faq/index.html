❓ Frequently Asked Questions

The 10 questions beginners ask most — answered clearly.

What is a stock (share)?

A stock is a small piece of ownership in a company. Own a share of DBS or Apple and you own a sliver of that business — entitled to a proportional slice of its profits (through dividends) and its growth (through the share price).

What does "momentum" mean in investing?

Momentum is the well-documented tendency of stocks that have been rising to keep rising for a while, and falling stocks to keep falling. MomentumRadar measures it across five timeframes and condenses it into a transparent 0–100 score. See the full beginner's guide.

Is the data on this site real?

Yes. Prices come from Yahoo Finance's public market data and the whole site is rebuilt automatically every trading day — the exact refresh time is stamped in the header and on every market page. If a market's refresh ever fails, we show the last good data clearly labelled with its date instead of pretending it's current.

Is a high Momentum Score a buy signal?

No. The score describes what the price has already done — it is not a prediction or a recommendation. Strong stocks can reverse sharply (momentum's known weakness). Use the score as a starting point for research, then check the company's financial reports linked on each stock card.

Is momentum investing risky?

Yes — all stock investing risks loss, and momentum has a specific extra risk: sharp reversals, where previous winners fall hardest. Read Risk Management 101 before acting on anything you see here.

What is a "good" Momentum Score?

80+ means strong, consistent gains across most of the five timeframes; 65–79 is good; around 50 is neutral; below 40 means the stock has been weakening. Just as important is the direction of the 90-day score chart — 85 and rising is a different story from 85 and falling.

How do I start investing in stocks?

Broad steps: build an emergency fund first; open an account with a regulated broker in your country; start small with diversified index funds; add individual stocks only once you understand the risks; never invest money you need soon. We can't advise on your personal situation — a licensed financial adviser can.

What's the difference between a stock and an ETF?

A stock is one company; an ETF is a basket of many, traded as a single share. ETFs give instant diversification at low cost, which is why they're usually the sensible core for beginners.

Why do share prices go up and down?

Supply and demand, driven by profits and expectations of future profits, interest rates, economic news, and investor emotion. In the short run sentiment dominates; over years, business performance does.

How much money do I need to start investing?

Less than most people think — many brokers have no minimum and offer fractional shares. What matters more than the amount is the habit, the diversification, and only investing money you can leave untouched for years.
