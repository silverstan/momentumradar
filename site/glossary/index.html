📖 Investor's Glossary

36 essential terms, plain English, no jargon.

Ask Price

The lowest price a seller is willing to accept for a stock. When you buy, you typically pay the ask (also called the offer price).

Bear Market

A fall of 20% or more from recent highs over a sustained period, usually accompanied by widespread pessimism.

Bid Price

The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay for a stock. The gap between bid and ask is the spread — a hidden cost of every trade.

Blue Chip Stock

Shares in a large, financially stable company with a long record of reliable performance — think Apple, HSBC, DBS or Nestlé.

Bull Market

A sustained period of rising prices, typically defined as a 20%+ gain from recent lows with strong investor confidence.

Capital Gain

The profit from selling a stock for more than you paid. Buy at $50, sell at $70, and the capital gain is $20 per share.

Correction

A 10–20% decline in an index from its recent peak. Corrections are a normal part of market cycles, even in long bull markets.

Dividend

A share of company profits paid to shareholders, usually quarterly or annually, expressed per share or as a yield percentage.

Diversification

Spreading money across many stocks, sectors and countries so no single failure can badly damage your portfolio.

Drawdown

The decline from a portfolio's peak to its lowest subsequent point. A key measure of how painful a strategy can get before it recovers.

Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's net profit divided by its share count — one of the most widely used measures of profitability.

ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

A fund holding a basket of stocks that trades like a single share. Instant diversification at low cost.

Fundamental Analysis

Judging a company's value from its financial statements, business model and management — the numbers behind the price.

Index

A measure of a group of stocks: S&P 500 (US), FTSE 100 (UK), Nikkei 225 (Japan), KLCI (Malaysia), VN-Index (Vietnam).

IPO (Initial Public Offering)

A private company selling shares to the public for the first time and listing on an exchange.

Liquidity

How easily a stock can be bought or sold without moving its price. Large caps are liquid; small emerging-market stocks may not be.

Market Capitalisation

Share price × number of shares — the total market value of a company.

Momentum

The tendency of rising stocks to keep rising and falling stocks to keep falling over medium horizons. The foundation of this site.

Momentum Crash

A sharp reversal in which previous winning stocks fall hardest — momentum investing's characteristic risk (e.g. 2009, March 2020).

Moving Average

The average closing price over a rolling window. The 50-day and 200-day averages are the most watched trend indicators.

Order Book

The live list of all outstanding buy and sell orders for a stock, with their prices and quantities.

P/E Ratio

Price divided by earnings per share. A P/E of 20 means paying $20 for each $1 of annual profit; higher usually implies higher expected growth.

Portfolio

Everything you own as investments — stocks, funds, bonds, cash. Constructing it well matters more than any single pick.

Price-to-Book Ratio

Share price versus the accounting value of the company's net assets. Below 1 means the market prices it under book value.

Rally

A period of sustained price rises, which can occur within both bull and bear markets.

Return on Equity (ROE)

Net income divided by shareholders' equity — how efficiently a company turns its capital into profit. Consistently above 15% is a quality marker.

Risk-Reward Ratio

Potential profit relative to potential loss. A 3:1 setup risks $1 to plausibly gain $3. Professionals size this before entering.

Sector

A group of companies in similar businesses: Technology, Financials, Healthcare, Energy, Consumer Staples and so on.

Share / Stock

A unit of ownership in a company, entitling you to a share of its profits and assets.

Short Selling

Selling borrowed shares hoping to buy them back cheaper. High risk, potentially unlimited losses — not for beginners.

Stop-Loss Order

An instruction to automatically sell if the price falls to your chosen level, capping your loss. A core risk-management tool.

Technical Analysis

Studying price charts, volume and indicators to gauge trends — the school of analysis momentum belongs to.

Trading Volume

Shares traded in a period. Rising prices on high volume signal conviction; on thin volume, less so.

Volatility

How much a price swings. Higher volatility means bigger potential gains and losses; we show 30-day annualised volatility on top picks.

Yield

Income return as a percentage of price. A $2 annual dividend on a $40 stock is a 5% yield.

52-Week High / Low

The highest and lowest prices of the past year. Distance from the 52-week high is a widely watched momentum indicator.
