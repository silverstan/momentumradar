What Is Momentum Investing? A Beginner's Complete Guide

⏱ 6 min read · Educational content, not financial advice

Momentum investing is built on a simple, well-documented observation: stocks that have performed strongly over the recent past tend, on average, to keep performing strongly for a while — and stocks that have been falling tend to keep falling. In finance research this is called the momentum effect, and it is one of the most persistent patterns ever documented in market data, studied across countries, decades and asset classes since Jegadeesh and Titman's landmark 1993 paper.

The core idea in one sentence

Buy strength, avoid weakness. Instead of hunting for "cheap" stocks that might recover (value investing), a momentum investor looks for stocks already in strong uptrends, on the theory that trends persist more often than they reverse over medium horizons of roughly 3 to 12 months.

Why does momentum exist?

Nobody knows for certain, but the leading explanations are behavioural. Investors underreact to new information at first — good news gets priced in slowly as it spreads, analysts revise forecasts gradually, and big institutions build positions over weeks, not minutes. Then, later in a trend, herding takes over as more people pile in simply because the price is rising. Both behaviours stretch a price move out over months, which is exactly what a momentum investor tries to ride.

What the evidence says — honestly

Long-run studies show momentum strategies have historically produced excess returns in most equity markets. But the same research is equally clear about the downside: momentum suffers from occasional momentum crashes. When a long trend suddenly reverses — as in early 2009 or March 2020 — the previous "winners" can fall harder and faster than the overall market. Momentum is not a free lunch; it is a trade-off between long stretches of decent performance and rare, sharp reversals.

These aren't enemies. Many professionals blend them, and for most beginners a diversified index fund remains the sensible core of a portfolio, with any momentum-based stock picking kept to a small, strictly risk-managed part.

Beginner takeaways

Momentum is real but not magic. It works until it doesn't, and the "doesn't" arrives suddenly. If you ever use momentum ideas with real money: keep positions small, diversify across markets and sectors, decide your exit before you enter, and never confuse a rising price with a good business. The two often overlap — and sometimes very much don't.
