Risk Management 101: How to Protect Your Investment Capital

⏱ 6 min read · Educational content, not financial advice

Every investor talks about making money. Professionals talk mostly about not losing it — because losses are asymmetric: a 50% loss needs a 100% gain just to get back to even. If you take only one page from this site into your investing life, make it this one.

Rule 1 — position sizing beats stock picking

The fastest way to ruin is concentration. A practical beginner's guideline: no single stock should be more than 5% of your portfolio, and for volatile momentum names many professionals use less. Sizing means a bad outcome in any one stock is an annoyance, not a catastrophe. Ask before every purchase: "If this falls 50%, does it change my life?" The answer must be no.

Rule 2 — diversify across more than just stocks

Owning ten technology momentum stocks is not diversification — they will rise and fall together. Real diversification spans sectors (tech, banks, healthcare, energy), regions (the 13 markets on this site move on different drivers), and asset classes (many investors hold index funds and cash alongside individual shares). Diversification is the only free lunch in finance: it lowers risk more than it lowers expected return.

Rule 3 — decide your exit before you enter

The worst investment decisions are made while losing money. Decide in advance what would make you sell: a price level (e.g. "if it falls 15% from my entry, I'm out"), a broken thesis ("if the momentum score drops below 50 for a month"), or a time limit. A stop-loss order automates the price version — your broker sells automatically at your predetermined level. Stops aren't perfect (prices can gap through them overnight), but an imperfect plan beats no plan.

Rule 4 — respect volatility

Each top pick on MomentumRadar shows 30-day annualised volatility. A stock with 60% volatility routinely swings several percent a day — completely normal for it, terrifying if you didn't expect it. High-volatility positions should be smaller positions. Panic-selling a normal fluctuation is one of the most expensive beginner mistakes.

Rule 5 — know momentum's specific risk

Momentum strategies carry a known failure mode: sharp reversals. The stocks that rose most steeply fall hardest when sentiment turns, as in 2008–09 and March 2020. If you follow momentum ideas: expect this, size for it, and treat a falling Momentum Score history (visible on every stock card) as information rather than an insult.

Rule 6 — only invest money you can leave alone

Money you'll need within a couple of years — rent, tuition, an emergency fund — does not belong in stocks. Being forced to sell during a downturn converts a temporary decline into a permanent loss. Build the emergency cushion first; invest what remains.

The psychology is the hard part

Most investing failures are behavioural, not analytical: chasing a stock after it doubled, averaging down on a loser to "fix" the entry price, checking prices hourly, selling everything at the bottom of a scary week. Rules 1–6 exist precisely to make your future, emotional self less dangerous. Write your rules down. Follow them mechanically. Nothing on this site is financial advice — but "have a plan before you need one" is as close to universal wisdom as investing gets.
